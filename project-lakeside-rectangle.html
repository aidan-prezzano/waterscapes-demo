Story

A pool that earns its view without fighting it.

The lot backed up to water — a quiet lake on the eastern edge of a Daphne neighborhood — and the clients wanted the pool to feel like it belonged to that landscape, not like it was dropped into it. One rectangle, oriented to the trees and water beyond. Dark concrete, not white.

We ran the pool fourteen meters along the view axis and built a low stone planter wall between the pool deck and the outdoor living area behind it. The wall handles a grade transition the lot needed anyway and gives the pool a visual frame from the house — you look through the planter, across the deck, and out to the water. The pool itself sits in the middle of that sequence.

The stamped concrete deck was poured in a single session and colored in a dark slate. The coping is a matching dark charcoal. On bright days the deck reads warm; on overcast days it nearly disappears into the background. That was the intention — the pool is the thing you see, not the concrete around it.

"We told Clay we wanted a pool that didn't look like it was trying too hard. He understood immediately." Tom & Angela M. — Clients

The pool is fourteen meters — a good honest lap length. The family uses it for morning swims and afternoon entertaining. The lake behind it catches the afternoon light differently every day. From inside the pool, you look east into that view without any fence or grade break interrupting the sight line.

Six months from permit to first swim. The lot now reads as one continuous outdoor room from the back door to the water's edge.
