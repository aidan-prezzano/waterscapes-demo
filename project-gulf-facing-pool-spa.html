Story

Water within water, facing the Gulf.

The clients had lived on this stretch of 30A for nineteen years and knew the light exactly. They wanted a pool that, at four in the afternoon, would read as a darker rectangle inside a larger, brighter one — the basin doubling the Gulf behind it.

We oriented the rectangle perfectly square to the waterline and set the spa flush with the deck at the pool's north edge, not as a separate element but as a raised continuation of the basin — two surfaces, one composition. Spa spill is a single grooved slot along the shared wall; you hear it before you see it. At rest, the two volumes read as one still plane.

The dark-slate plaster was the most careful decision on the project. We built three eight-foot sample panels on site and lived with them for a full day across different skies before committing. The finish reads nearly black at noon, dense blue in storm light, and a warm bronze at dusk. The coping is honed keystone, flush-set to zero, so the eye sees no frame.

"We asked for a pool that looked like it had been here since the house was built. Shoreline gave us one that looks like it had been here since the Gulf was carved." Helen & Asher M. — Clients

Symmetry is the easier half of the problem. The harder half is making symmetry feel inhabited. We offset the two entry benches by a single tile width and set them at different depths — six inches on the near side, twelve on the far — so the eye reads the basin as balanced but the body reads it as lived-in. Almost no one notices until they've swum three times.

Construction took twenty months. The spa spill tuning required four revisions; we rebuilt the weir profile in situ until the sound sat below the Gulf's own wash. The final commissioning happened on a still morning in June with no wind — the day the house and the pool first held the same sound.
