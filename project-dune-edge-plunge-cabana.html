Story

A meditation on quiet, not spectacle.

The clients had grown children and a dune-edge lot they had owned for thirty years. They did not want a pool for entertaining. They wanted a cold plunge, an integrated spa, and a cabana quiet enough to hear the Gulf through.

We set the plunge and spa as two small, twin volumes cut into the dune line, oriented perpendicular to the water so neither is the first thing you see from the house. The cabana sits between them on a low platform, four posts, a flat roof, one long bench. A single outdoor shower at the cabana's far end. Nothing else.

The finish is a rough-cast cement in an oyster tone matched to the shell fragments that wash up on the beach below. Coping is a single unmortared course of reclaimed oyster-tabby block laid dry — flush-set but not committed, so in fifty years the next owner can pull it out and start over without demolition. The plunge sits at eight-degrees-Celsius; the spa sits at thirty-eight. The gradient from one to the other is the whole project.

"We had been on this dune for three decades and never added anything to it. This is the first thing we have ever built that we think belongs here." James & Iris L. — Clients

The dune was the hardest client. We dug no deeper than the existing grade required and kept all excavation material on site for the new planting bed. Every plant is a native species — sea oats, railroad vine, beach morning glory — sourced from a nursery forty miles east and installed the same week it was harvested. The dune profile at the end of construction reads, from a hundred feet, identical to the profile we started with.

Construction took fourteen months. The cabana roof was poured in a single night to avoid a cold joint. The plunge refrigeration unit was a small mechanical feat — ducted under the dune and vented through the existing house mechanical stack, so no new equipment reads from the beach.
