Story

A pool that ends where the Gulf begins.

The site gave us one clear instruction: whatever we built had to dissolve. A narrow bluff lot, thirty feet above the waterline, with a west-facing view down the length of Perdido Key. The pool would be the last thing the house owned before the horizon took over.

We oriented a thirty-foot infinity run on axis with the afternoon light. The negative edge was ground to a six-millimeter weir — thin enough that the water surface and the Gulf surface blur for most of an hour around five o'clock. The catch basin is buried below grade so nothing interrupts the meeting of the two planes. From the deck, from the kitchen window, from the bedroom above, the reading is the same: the water runs out of the pool into the Gulf itself.

Coping is a single, unbroken run of limestone — honed, not polished — matched to the house's cladding. The finish below the waterline is a graphite pebble, dark enough to read as a second horizon at dusk. Nothing about the pool competes with what lies beyond.

"The first evening we swam, we watched the sun set over the Gulf with no edge between the water we were in and the water we were looking at. That is exactly what we asked for." David & Hannah T. — Clients

The hardest detail was the basin. A negative edge fails if the catch volume is wrong by half an inch or if the return pumps overrun at the wrong moment. We specified a variable-frequency drive tied to a surface-level sensor and tuned the response curve on site over three days before we handed it off. It is the quietest piece of equipment in the mechanical room.

Construction took eighteen months. A hurricane passed during the pour; we lost ten days and one load of rebar. The shell survived intact. We poured the infinity wall separately, with a keyed cold joint engineered by Walter P Moore, and tested the weir for a month before finishing the deck.
